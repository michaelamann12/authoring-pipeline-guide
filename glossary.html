Question types & response formatsWhat students are asked to do

The one to know: LCQ vs. TDQ. An LCQ asks "what does the text say?" — the answer is right there to point to. A TDQ asks "what does it mean / how do you know?" — the answer is grounded in the text but takes inference or analysis.
TermWhat it means
LCQLiteral Comprehension Question. The answer is stated explicitly in the text; students point to it and quote it back. Used for factual recall, often at lower grades.
TDQText-Dependent Question. Requires inference, interpretation, or analysis — grounded in the text but not stated outright. Builds analytical thinking.
lcq_no_reader
tdq_no_reader		The same LCQ/TDQ pedagogy for activities with no reader panel — the source (image, video, graphic novel) is viewed outside the activity. Distinct type labels.
HighlightStudents select/mark specific evidence in the text — they find and highlight it rather than explain it.
MCMultiple Choice. Four options (A–D), pick one. Some curricula add a short rationale for each wrong answer (a distractor).
BrainstormA pre-writing scaffold — students jot 2–3 ideas before a structured write. Never the graded deliverable itself.
ClaimA one-sentence position students will defend in the paired write ("topic statement" in G4–5 / Arts & Letters). Targets the same focus as the write.
QuickWriteA one-paragraph written assessment using one piece of evidence to support a claim.
ShortWriteA one-paragraph written assessment using two pieces of evidence — for deeper or multi-source analysis.
LongWriteA multi-paragraph essay (often 3–5 paragraphs) with multiple pieces of evidence, sometimes across texts. For complex/synthesis tasks.
ShortSummaryA 1–3 sentence objective summary of the text's gist — no claim, no interpretation, stands alone.
Explanatory (v-2)The "explain/inform" flavor of QuickWrite/ShortWrite — students explain rather than argue; no claim structure, scored on a different rubric.
ExtensionAn open-ended reflection prompt at the end of an activity — no single right answer; follows curriculum-specific patterns.
GrammarA sentence-level grammar/mechanics question (Fishtank-specific, post-writing).
VOC / VocabularyA question targeting a word's meaning in context.

Concepts & fieldsThe pieces of an activity

TermWhat it means
ObjectiveWhat students will learn or do this lesson — the headline, written "Students will analyze/identify/explain…"
KUKey Understandings. 1–3 bullets starting "Students can…" — the observable skills they'll show by the end, specific to this text and task.
TTTransferable Takeaway. A one-sentence, text-agnostic statement of the analytical move practiced — abstract enough to apply to other texts on the same skill.
ULCUnit-Level Connection. 1–2 sentences tying this lesson to the broader unit's question, skill, or theme.
Activity TypeThe pedagogical frame (First Read, Close Read, Visual Analysis, Grammar…) that sets the question sequence and cognitive demand.
EQEssential Question. The unit/curriculum-level question driving the analytical work across lessons.
DQDriving Question. A guided-practice question (Section 1) that scaffolds toward the assessment.
Target Task /
Assessment Task		The summative question (Section 2) where students show full understanding — the Claim + Write, MC, etc.
PIAPedagogical Intent Analysis. The breakdown of what analytical moves and frameworks the activity is built to teach.
CriteriaOne-sentence statements describing what a successful answer looks like (on every non-MC question). Must mirror the question exactly.
AlignmentHow well a question/criterion/activity matches the stated objective and standards.
First ReadA literal-comprehension pass to establish what the text actually says, before analysis.
Close ReadAn analytical pass focused on craft, language, or interpretation.
Reader Content /
Reader Panel		The excerpt shown to students in the activity. Some activities are "no reader" (source viewed elsewhere).
Match ReportClaude's design rationale for a draft — which exemplars it used and why it built the questions the way it did.

Lesson IDsReading a ticket name

Every ticket is a lesson, named like this:

TX_BBO_XX_G5_1.0_7_v1
  • TX_BBO_XX — curriculum code (here, Bluebonnet)
  • G5 — grade level (G3–G10)
  • 1.0 — module/unit (a letter can appear for split units, e.g. 8.A)
  • 7 — lesson number within the module
  • v1 — version (bumps when a lesson is revised)
This is how Claude knows what to build. The lesson ID on the ticket points to the matching row in the module plan — so a correct ID is what connects everything.
